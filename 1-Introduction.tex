\section{Introduction}
\label{Sec:Introduction}

Termination analysis of infinite-state systems is a fundamental problem in 
formal verification, with applications in static analysis, compiler 
optimisation, and the verification of safety-critical software.
Proving termination and nontermination over LIA is generally undecidable~\cite{DBLP:conf/cav/Braverman06}, 
yet many practical techniques succeed on large classes of real-world 
programs, though the problem remains challenging in general.
Modern techniques typically treat termination and nontermination as separate 
problems, since each requires a different witness.

State-of-the-art termination techniques centre around the synthesis of 
ranking functions for the transition relation~\cite{DBLP:conf/vmcai/PodelskiR04,DBLP:conf/cav/KuraUH20,DBLP:journals/jacm/Ben-AmramG14,DBLP:conf/cav/HeizmannHP14,DBLP:journals/jar/GieslABEFFHOPSS17,DBLP:conf/esop/SaritaSGSV26,DBLP:conf/sas/Ben-AmramDG19}.
The existence of a ranking function for the transition relation witnesses 
termination by providing a measure that strictly decreases along every 
execution.
Alternative approaches are based on the computability of closed forms~\cite{DBLP:conf/sas/FrohnHG20,DBLP:journals/fmsd/HarkFG25}, 
fixpoint computation~\cite{DBLP:journals/pacmpl/UnnoTGK23} and the synthesis of disjunctively well-founded 
transition invariants~\cite{Transition-Invariants-Revisited-CAV26,DBLP:conf/pldi/CookPR06,DBLP:conf/cav/KroeningSTW10,DBLP:conf/tacas/TsitovichSWK11}.

Nontermination analysis typically relies on different verification 
techniques. One of the most prominent is the construction of recurrent 
sets — sets of states from which execution cannot 
escape~\cite{DBLP:conf/sas/Ben-AmramDG19,DBLP:conf/tacas/ChenCFNO14,DBLP:conf/cade/FrohnG22,DBLP:conf/tacas/BrockschmidtCIK16,DBLP:conf/foveoos/BrockschmidtSOG11,DBLP:conf/cav/LarrazNORR14}.
An alternative family of techniques detects nontermination by identifying 
lasso-shaped executions — finite paths leading to a loop that can be 
repeated indefinitely~\cite{DBLP:journals/entcs/BiereAS02,DBLP:conf/popl/GuptaHMRX08,DBLP:conf/tacas/LeikeH18}.


Most existing approaches treat termination and nontermination in isolation, 
and transition invariant synthesis has received comparatively less attention 
than ranking function methods despite its greater generality. 
In this work, we address both limitations: we present a unified procedure 
that proves both termination and nontermination.
This is done by extending and optimizing the safety-based nontermination analysis~\cite{DBLP:conf/tacas/ChenCFNO14}, allowing it to 
prove termination and nontermination of transition systems.
For this, we develop a new  interpolation-based method for synthesising disjunctively well-founded 
transition invariants.
This approach constructs invariants based on the termination conditions.
Algorithm detects terminating traces, and constructs an interpolant, that captures the reason why the trace necessarily terminates.
Additionally, constructed transition invariant candidates are used to detect potentially nonterminating states that are 
reachable within the original system.
These potentially nonterminating states guide the termination analysis, for more efficient construction of transition invariants 
and detection of recurrent sets.


Our new technique was implemented within the Golem CHC solver. 
We evaluated our technique over the Integer Transition Systems benchmarks from the Termination Competition. 
The combination of termination and nontermination approaches demonstrated significant improvement for nontermination analysis, 
compared to the original approach, while being able to solve a large number of terminating instances.
The comparison with winners of Termination Competition 2025 (KoAT, LoAT~\cite{10.1007/978-3-031-90660-2_13}, T2~\cite{DBLP:conf/tacas/BrockschmidtCIK16}) also demonstrated that the developed approach
is competitive with the state-of-the-art tools, and is able to solve unique instances, including instances
that were never solved in the history of Termination competition.
