our termination approach.

\subsection{Implementation}


We implemented Algorithms~\ref{Alg:SNA}, \ref{Alg:SNA_Term}, and \ref{Alg:SNA_Refined} inside of the \golem~\cite{DBLP:conf/cav/BlichaBS23}; 
we refer to these implementations as \sna, \snaTerm and \snaOpt.
\sna handles trivial termination cases during preprocessing, such as the absence of initial states or the 
absence of transitions from initial states.
\golem supports multiple different safety verification procedures,
such as Property Directed Reachability~\cite{DBLP:journals/fac/BradleyM08} (PDR), Transition Power Abstraction~\cite{DBLP:conf/tacas/BlichaFHS22} (TPA) and Interpolation-based Model Checking~\cite{DBLP:conf/cav/McMillan03}.
Additionally, we use quantifier elimination, already implemented in \golem.
% \snaOpt with different safety verification engines was able to solve different 
% termination problems uniquely.
% Quantifier Elimination is produced by executing Model Based Projections, untill the whole formula is covere

For SMT queries and interpolant construction, our implementation uses the \opensmt SMT solver~\cite{DBLP:conf/sat/HyvarinenMAS16}.
\opensmt is integrated within \golem and provides native support for interpolation.


\subsection{Evaluation}


We compare \snaOpt against \sna and \snaTerm, to evaluate the effect of combination of
the termination and nontermination techniques.
Additionally, we evaluate \snaOpt against several state-of-the-art tools: 
\LoAT~\cite{10.1007/978-3-031-90660-2_13} (version eacb74b), \KoAT~\cite{10.1007/978-3-031-90660-2_13} 
(version 41cb681), \Tt~\cite{DBLP:conf/tacas/BrockschmidtCIK16} (version 10f1373). 
Evaluation focuses on termination analysis of Integer Transition Systems. 
Experiments were conducted on Ubuntu 20.04 machine with an AMD EPYC 7452 32-core processor and 8x32 GiB RAM. 
Our evaluation addresses the following research questions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1:} How does extending \sna with the termination procedure affect verification performance?
\item \textbf{RQ2:} How does \snaOpt perform compared to state-of-the-art tools?
\end{itemize}

The benchmark suite of Integer Transition Systems is taken from the Termination Competition. 
Overall there are 1180 benchmarks (538 nonterminating, 623 terminating, and 19 unknown).
The evaluation was run with 120 seconds timeout (increase of the timeout almost does not influence the evaluation).

Table~\ref{tab:internal} gives the experimental results for \snaOpt, \sna, and \snaTerm. 
Our results indicate that \snaOpt consistently outperforms both \sna and \snaTerm for Terminating 
and Nonterminating instances. 
We attribute this to the usefulness of creating "subproblems" in the \snaOpt, that allows the algorithm to focus
on the termination of specific subproblem instead of considering the whole system.
This benefits recurrent set detection, by focusing the analysis on specific states not covered by the transition invariant.
It also benefits termination proving, by enriching the transition invariant with additional disjuncts 
that cover different regions of the reachable state space.
In answer to \textbf{RQ1}, the combination of termination and nontermination reasoning in 
\snaOpt yields significantly improved performance across both problem classes.
Notably, during experimentation we noticed that \snaOpt produces different outputs depending on the underlying 
Safety Verification engine.
For example, the most efficient results were produced using TPA \snaOpt - 785 instances solved. 
\snaOpt with PDR solves less instances (781), however it can solve 17 benchmarks uniquely compared to the TPA.

Figure~\ref{fig:stateOfArtComp} shows a scatter plot comparing \snaOpt against state-of-the-art termination analysers.
Orange squares denote nonterminating instances; blue crosses denote terminating instances.
Points above the diagonal indicate instances where \snaOpt is faster than the competitor.
\snaOpt is able to solve 785 benchmarks, with \Tt solving 1044, \KoAT 604, and \LoAT 527 instances.
As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:stateOfArtComp}, \snaOpt is competitive with state-of-the-art termination analysers.
Among all tools, \snaOpt uniquely solves 8 instances.
Notably, 2 of these — \texttt{juLinkedListCreateAddAll.jar-obl-11} and \texttt{juLinkedListCreateAddAllAt.jar-obl-17} — belong to the 
19 previously unsolved instances in the Termination Competition, for which \snaOpt finds recurrent sets proving nontermination.
In answer to \textbf{RQ2}, \snaOpt is competitive with state-of-the-art tools overall and uniquely solves instances previously unsolved in the Termination Competition, 
demonstrating the practical value of the interpolation-based transition invariant approach.
