our termination approach.

~\subsection{Implementation}


We implemented Algorithms~\ref{Alg:SNA},\ref{Alg:SNA_Term} and \ref{Alg:SNA_Refined} in the \golem CHC 
solver~\cite{DBLP:conf/cav/BlichaBS23}; we refer to this implementations as \sna, \snaTerm and \snaOpt.
\golem is a well-known CHC solver which we extended to be able to analyse termination of systems.
Particularly we picked \golem, as it supports multiple different safety verification procedures,
such as Property Directed Reachability, Transition Power Abstraction and Interpolation-based Model Checking.
Interestingly, \snaOpt with different safety verification engines was able to solve different 
termination problems uniquely.
Additionally, \golem has its own procedures for Quantifier Elimination and Model Based Projection,
which we were able to use natively for our technique.
% Quantifier Elimination is produced by executing Model Based Projections, untill the whole formula is covere

For SMT queries used in the algorithm and the construction of interpolants our algorithm uses  \opensmt SMT solver~\cite{DBLP:conf/sat/HyvarinenMAS16}.
According to the SMT Competition, it is one of the best SMT solvers in LIA-Lin category, and 
additionally, \opensmt supports multiple different interpolation procedures with different strength of interpolant, which 
slightly influence the termination analysis.



~\subsection{Evaluation}


We compare \snaOpt to \sna and \snaTerm algorithms, evaluating the benefit of combining
the termination and nontermination techniques.
Additionally, we evaluate \snaOpt against several state-of-the-art tools: 
\LoAT~\cite{10.1007/978-3-031-90660-2_13} (version eacb74b), \KoAT~\cite{10.1007/978-3-031-90660-2_13} 
(version 41cb681), \Tt~\cite{DBLP:conf/tacas/BrockschmidtCIK16} (version 10f1373). 
Evaluation focuses on termination analysis of Integer Transition Systems. 
Experiments were conducted on Ubuntu 20.04 machine with an AMD EPYC 7452 32-core processor and 8x32 GiB RAM. 
Our evaluation addresses the following research questions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1:} How does the extension of SNA with Termination procedure influences the performance of the verification?
\item \textbf{RQ2:} How does \snaOpt perform against state-of-the-art tools?
\end{itemize}

The benchmark suite of Integer Transition Systems is taken from the Termination Competition. 
Overall there are 1222 benchmarks (538 nonterminating, 665 terminating and 19 unknown).


\begin{figure}[t]

    \label{fig:golemComp}

Figure~\ref{fig:golemComp} gives two plots comparing \snaOpt to \sna and \snaTerm. 
Our results indicate that \snaOpt consistently outperforms both \sna and \snaTerm for Terminating 
and Nonterminating instances. 
We contribute this to the usefuleness of creating "subproblems" in the \snaOpt, that allows
to concentrate on the termination of specific subproblem instead of considering the whole system.
This is benefitial in finding a recurrent set, as it allows to concentrate on some
specific state not covered by transition invariant.
And it is also benefitial for the purposes of the termination, as it allows to diversify
and enrich the transition invariant with additional disjuncts, covering some different 
subspace of the reachable state space.
To answer \textbf{RQ1}, we conclude that the combination of termination and nontermination 
techniques in \snaOpt leads to significantly improved performance, both for termination and 
nontermination.

\snaOpt is able to solve 796 benchmarks, with \Tt solving 1044, \KoAT 604, and \LoAT 527 instances.
As it can be seen in the graphs, \snaOpt is competitive with other state of the art techniques 
for the termination analysis.
Compared to other state-of-the-art tools \snaOpt is able to analyse 8 instances uniquely, importantly
verifying 2 instances (juLinkedListCreateAddAll.jar-obl-11, juLinkedListCreateAddAllAt.jar-obl-17)
out of 19 which were never verified before by any other tool that participated in Termination Competition,
finding recurrent sets that prove nontermination of those benchmarks.
