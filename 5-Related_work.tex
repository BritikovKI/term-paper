Termination and nontermination analysis are very active areas of research. We will discuss techniques,
related to Interpolation-based Termination Analysis below.

\textbf{Termination.} There exist many different techniques for the termination analysis of software.
One of the most developed and used techniques iss the synthesis of the ranking functions for the transition
relation, which allows to prove termination~\cite{DBLP:conf/vmcai/PodelskiR04,DBLP:conf/cav/KuraUH20,DBLP:journals/jacm/Ben-AmramG14,DBLP:conf/cav/HeizmannHP14,DBLP:journals/jar/GieslABEFFHOPSS17,DBLP:conf/esop/SaritaSGSV26,DBLP:conf/sas/Ben-AmramDG19}. 
State of the art techniques are centered on the construction of multiphase or lexicographic ranking functions.
These complex techniques significantly broaden the class of programs that ranking functions can handle.
However, since they reason on the original transitions, the complexity of original system can make construction of ranking 
function significantly more complex, or even impossible. 

Another techniques, which allows to efficiently prove termination are fixpoint computation~\cite{DBLP:journals/pacmpl/UnnoTGK23} and 
the computation of closed forms~\cite{DBLP:conf/sas/FrohnHG20,DBLP:journals/fmsd/HarkFG25}.

However, there exists another set of techniques which can prove termination that are based on the construction of well-founded transition
invariants~\cite{DBLP:conf/pldi/CookPR06,DBLP:conf/cav/KroeningSTW10,DBLP:conf/tacas/TsitovichSWK11}.
These approaches allow to approximate the behaviour of the transition system, and to significantly simplify the transition relation,
overapproximating any possible transition trace.
However, these approaches have a core problem, which is the approach for the synthesis of transition invariant.
Listed techniques were based on the synthesis of transition invariants via template synthesis, not centered around actual termination constraints
or terminating traces.

\textbf{Nontermination.} Nontermination analysis typically relies on different verification 
techniques. One of the most prominent is the construction of recurrent 
sets — sets of states from which execution cannot 
escape~\cite{DBLP:conf/sas/Ben-AmramDG19,DBLP:conf/tacas/ChenCFNO14,DBLP:conf/cade/FrohnG22,DBLP:conf/tacas/BrockschmidtCIK16,DBLP:conf/foveoos/BrockschmidtSOG11,DBLP:conf/cav/LarrazNORR14}.
An alternative family of techniques detects nontermination by identifying 
lasso-shaped executions — finite paths leading to a loop that can be 
repeated indefinitely~\cite{DBLP:journals/entcs/BiereAS02,DBLP:conf/popl/GuptaHMRX08,DBLP:conf/tacas/LeikeH18}.
